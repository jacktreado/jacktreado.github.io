About Me

About Me

I am a PhD candidate at Yale University in the Mechanical Engineering and Materials Science department working with Corey O'Hern. I use computational physics to construct physical models of disordered biological systems, from packing in the cores of single protein molecules to jamming and collective migration in cells and tissues. My work blends soft condensed matter, statistical physics, image analysis and computational geometry.

For more details, see my CV. You can reach me at john.treado@yale.edu.

Research

Simple Models | Complex Behavior

My work involves creating coarse-grained models of biological systems. A good example is shown here: a crawling cell built with the deformable particle model. The cell crawls by protruding its perimeter in the direction of motion, which resembles the crawling of real cells, but lacks specific microscopic details like organelles or explicit signaling pathways.

This approach allows us to ask fundamental questions about a wide variety of biological pheneomena, from the arrangement of molecules in protein cores to collective migration during tissue development.

Jamming in globular protein cores

Globular proteins rely on a core of hydrophobic amino acids for thermodynamic stability. It's been known since the dawn of x-ray crystallography that these structures are densely packed, but our recent work has shown that crystal structures resemble jammed packings of hard particles shaped like amino acids. Interestingly, cryogenic (~77 K) crystal structures resemble packings prepared with athermal protocols, but structures resolved at room temperaure by NMR spectroscopy resemble denser packings formed by thermal protocols like the one shown here. We conjecture that NMR structures in aqueous, room-temperature solutions allow core amino acids to find denser configurations.

Deformability and response

Complex soft solids like emulsions (e.g. mayonnaise), foams (e.g. capuccino froth) and tissues (e.g. your epidermis) are composed of inherently deformable objects like droplets, bubbles or cells, yet how deformability affects collective behavior is poorly understood. In our recent paper, we studied the effect of single-particle deformability on global vibrational and mechanical response near jamming. We found that stiff, weakly deformable particles reach a rigid-particle limit, but increasing deformability leads to anomalous normal mode spectra and shear moduli.

On the right are some examples of normal modes found in jammed packings of highly deformable particles. These packings contain a few "quartic modes" which are higher-order and much floppier than standard, quadratic modes. Floppy quartic modes can be either extended (black, left) or localized (black, right), while the quadratic modes (red and blue) vary in extent, but predominantly change particle shape.




In Progress

Mesophyll development

The spongy mesophyll tissue, a complex porous tissue found within leaves and flowers, captures atmospheric carbon during photosynthesis and provides important structural stability. But how does this tissue develop? We study this mechanism as a self-assembly process, where an initially dense tissue slowly gains porosity due to differential growth between the boundaries and the cells.

We investigate the development and structure of spongy mesophyll in collaboration with Adam Roddy (Florida International University, Biological Sciences) and Craig Brodersen (Yale, School of the Environment).

Collective cell migration

Cells typically don't stay put, rather they search for nutrients, grow, divide and work together to maintain the fabric of our tissues and organs. In the case of cancer, some cells can work collectively to invade other tissues. I am interested in how the physical properties of crawling cells, such as their shape, stiffness and energy budget, affect their function and collective behavior.

For example, shown here is a simple, dense collective of actively crawling cells. In this active glass, how much does a cell's shape (shown as color) determine its mobility, and vice versa? This question is particularly important during tumor growth and invasion.

In collaboration with Claudia Fischbach (Cornell University, Biomedical Engineering), we study the effect of mechanical heterogeneity on the invasiveness of breast cancer.

Clogging of deformable particles

Arrested flow in the presence of confinement (clogging) is important throughout nature and industry. While there is a long history of studying clogging of hard particles (think grains in a silo), less attention has been payed to clogging of deformable objects. In particular, if deformable particles form a clog, can't they always change shape to resume flow? We have found that while long-lasting clogs can form, transient clogs with complex, intermittent flowing states become more ubiquitous with increasing particle deformability.

Our work is done in colaboration with Eric Weeks (Emory University, Physics).
